The Crow's Nest
National Historic Site Â· Est. 1942
A Living Museum of the Battle of the Atlantic
Perched on the third and fourth floors of a brick warehouse on Water Street â€” up a famous 59-step staircase â€” the Crow's Nest Officers' Club is one of the most remarkable military heritage sites in Canada. Designated a National Historic Site in 2011, it remains an active club and a living museum, virtually unchanged since the Second World War. Visitors, guests, and serving members are all welcome.

In 1942, at the height of the Battle of the Atlantic, St. John's was the home port of the Newfoundland Escort Force â€” the handover point for the critical supply convoys that kept Britain in the war. Thousands of Allied naval and merchant navy officers passed through the harbour, often exhausted after harrowing crossings under constant U-boat threat.

Captain Edward Rollo Mainguy, RCN, Captain (Destroyers) of the Newfoundland Escort Force, recognized that these officers needed somewhere ashore to decompress, build camaraderie, and develop the trust that would make them more effective at sea. On 27 January 1942, he opened the Seagoing Officers' Club on the top floor of the Old Butler Building, a warehouse built after St. John's Great Fire of 1892.

The name "Crow's Nest" came naturally â€” climbing those 59 steep steps to the fourth floor, with its sweeping view of the harbour and the Narrows, felt exactly like ascending to a ship's crow's nest. Club lore also credits Lieutenant Colonel Pete Stevens, who reportedly took one look and declared it "a snug little crow's nest."

For the remainder of the war, the Crow's Nest served as a sanctuary for Allied officers from more than a dozen nations. Between convoy runs, they gathered around the fireplace, shared stories of victory and loss over a drink, and enjoyed a home-cooked meal â€” a rare comfort in wartime.

The walls themselves became a canvas. Sailors began leaving handwritten messages, drawings, and mementos wherever they could find space. Captain Mainguy eventually gave each ship's company four square feet of wall to decorate as they saw fit. Much of this original wartime art â€” including striking gunshield paintings â€” remains on the walls today, exactly where it was placed over eighty years ago.

These are not just decorations. For many of those young men, it was the last mark they left before heading out to sea, never knowing if they would return. The walls of the Crow's Nest are a quiet and powerful memorial to their courage.

The club's most striking artefact is a German U-boat periscope that rises through the ceiling of the main room and out through the roof. It is one of only eight known-to-exist U-boat periscopes in the world, and one of only two in North America.

The periscope belonged to U-190, a Type IXC/40 submarine. On 16 April 1945, U-190 torpedoed and sank HMCS Esquimalt in the approaches to Halifax harbour â€” the last RCN warship lost in the war. After Germany's surrender, U-190 was ordered to surface and sailed into Bay Bulls, Newfoundland, where she surrendered on 12 May 1945.

The periscope was salvaged and installed in the Crow's Nest, where it still provides a working view of St. John's Harbour and the Narrows â€” a fitting transformation from an instrument of war into a lookout over the peaceful harbour it once threatened.

The Seagoing Officers' Club closed on 13 June 1945. But with thousands of Newfoundlanders returning from overseas, there was a strong desire to preserve the space. In June 1946, it reopened as the Crow's Nest Officers' Club, and has operated continuously ever since.

Over the decades, the club has evolved from an all-male, seagoing officers' club to a mixed-gender, tri-service, all-ranks club that also welcomes civilians. What has not changed is the atmosphere. Veterans who returned decades later said it looked exactly the same â€” the ultimate compliment.

The Crow's Nest Military Artifacts Association, a registered charity, conserves, preserves, and displays the hundreds of wartime artefacts. The club is recognized as a museum by the Museum Association of Newfoundland and Labrador and has received the Manning Award for Excellence in the Public Presentation of Historic Places.

It remains a place of pilgrimage. Visitors sometimes arrive because a father or grandfather served in Newfoundland during the war, and find something on the walls connected to their family story. These moments can be deeply moving.
